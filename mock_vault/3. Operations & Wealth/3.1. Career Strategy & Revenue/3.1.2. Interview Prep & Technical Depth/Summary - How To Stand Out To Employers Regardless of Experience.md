## Key Outcomes

A panel featuring Fordham Foundry, career services, and startup founders revealed that networking and direct outreach significantly outperform traditional job boards for startup positions. Most startup jobs are never publicly posted, instead circulating through founder networks and warm referrals. Success requires demonstrating speed, cultural fit, and authentic value proposition through creative approaches like product critiques, bug reports, or direct founder outreach.

## How to Get a Job at a Startup

### What Founders Look For

Speed and adaptability are critical startup qualities. Indicators include:

- Active campus involvement while maintaining strong academics
- Side projects or volunteer work alongside day jobs
- Quick responsiveness and ability to adjust to rapid changes

Cultural and mission alignment matters more than traditional credentials. Lived experience with the problem being solved is considered a "superpower." Sustainability and match between candidate and organization prevent early turnover.

### The Hidden Job Market Reality

Most startup positions are never posted on job boards. Founders typically:

- Create job descriptions and circulate through personal networks
- Post on LinkedIn linking to Notion or web-hosted descriptions
- Prioritize saving money over paid job board listings

Recruiters spend only 6 seconds scanning resumes, making first impressions and clear positioning critical.

### Networking Strategies That Work

Warm referrals dramatically increase hiring chances. Effective approaches:

- Ask connections for LinkedIn introductions to startup employees
- Attend startup ecosystem events (accelerators, happy hours, newsletters)
- Build relationships over time — networking connections may convert to opportunities 1-2 years later

The startup founder network is highly interconnected. Founders actively reference-check candidates through their networks and support each other's ventures.

## Unconventional Methods That Work

### Beyond the DM: Creative Attention-Grabbing Methods

> [!warning] Transcript Note
> The live capture paused approximately here. The panel continued discussing creative outreach strategies that went well beyond direct messaging — the notes below reflect their stated direction plus synthesized extensions of the same philosophy.

The panel emphasized that the DM is just the lowest bar of personalized outreach. The founders who responded most warmly were to candidates who had already done something visible and interesting *before* the message arrived — so that the DM felt like a follow-up, not a cold pitch. The frame: **make them aware of you first, then reach out.**

#### Make Something That References Them Specifically

- **Build a micro-tool for their problem:** If they're a FinTech startup, scrape their public pricing page and send them a Python script with an insight about their competitor pricing gaps. If they're in HR tech, build a tiny demo agent that does something in their domain.
- **Write a public teardown of their product:** Publish a LinkedIn post or a short blog article doing a detailed, respectful product critique — what works, what doesn't, what you'd do differently. Tag the founder. This is searchable, shareable, and shows competence without asking for anything.
- **Record a Loom walkthrough:** A 2-3 minute screen recording walking through a specific UX issue or feature idea beats a wall of text every time. It shows personality, communication skills, and effort simultaneously.
- **Redesign something and post it publicly:** Don't just email a mockup — post the redesign publicly (on X/Twitter, LinkedIn, or Dribbble), tag the company, and *then* send the DM referencing the post. Social proof converts curiosity into conversations.

#### Show Up Where They Actually Are

- **Attend their demo days and office hours:** YC, Fordham Foundry, a16z, and other accelerators run open demo days. Show up, ask a genuine question during Q&A, and introduce yourself afterward in person. Physical presence is irreplaceable.
- **Engage deeply with their content for weeks *before* reaching out:** Leave genuinely insightful comments on their LinkedIn posts and X threads over several weeks. By the time you send a DM, you're not a stranger — you're the person who always has something smart to say.
- **Become a power user of their product:** Sign up, use it obsessively, and generate real feedback. Founders read detailed product reviews from real users. Referencing your own usage in a DM is instantly more credible than anything else.

#### Pull Stunts That Are On-Brand

- **Physical mail in a digital world:** A well-designed one-page pitch card or handwritten note sent to their office is now absurdly rare and therefore memorable. Make it look like it belongs at their company.
- **Contribute to their open source:** Submit a real PR to their public GitHub repo. Even a small, clean fix signals technical competence and that you actually looked at their codebase.
- **Create a case study about their problem space:** Write up how you would architect a solution to a challenge they're publicly working on. Post it as an article, send it as a PDF. Founders are competitive — they want to see how you think about *their* space.
- **Reference-check yourself into the room:** Ask a mutual contact — even a weak-tie acquaintance — to send a one-line intro email before you reach out. A "Hey, my friend [Name] wanted to connect with you about [X]" converts a cold DM into a warm one instantly.

#### Academic Lab Outreach (The PI Strategy)
- **Read the Lab's Recent Papers:** Before reaching out to a Principal Investigator (PI), read their last 2-3 papers. Reference a specific methodology or result in your outreach. PIs are academics; they value intellectual curiosity and deep reading over general enthusiasm.
- **Identify Technical Gaps:** Many academic labs have brilliant researchers but lack strong software engineering infrastructure. Position yourself as the "Engineering Bridge" — the one who can turn their research into robust, deployable code or agentic workflows.
- **Reach out to PhD Students/Post-docs first:** They are often more accessible than the PI. Ask them about their current technical bottlenecks. Use that information to tailor your pitch to the PI.

### Product-Focused Approaches

Demonstrate value by improving their product:

- Identify bugs or UX issues on their website and propose fixes
- Create redesigned UI mockups (e.g., Spotify player redesign led to full-time hire with stock options)
- Build Canva presentations showing landing page improvements with screenshots
- Offer to be a product tester and provide detailed feedback

### Creative Outreach Tactics

Examples that have successfully landed jobs:

- Send Momofuku cookies to the office with your name if you know it's their company culture
- Use Wayback Machine to find old contact information from founders' early blogs
- Cold call founders directly with a voice pitch instead of waiting for resume review

### Go-To-Market Contributions

Non-technical candidates can provide immediate value. With AI making product building easier through vibe coding, startups need help with:

- Market strategy and user acquisition
- Customer testimonials and use case development
- Participant recruitment for product testing

## How to DM Founders Effectively

### What Gets Responses

Specific, value-driven messages outperform generic outreach. Successful DMs:

- Establish clear connection points (alumni status, shared mission passion)
- State specific interest and time request (10 minutes to discuss X)
- Demonstrate research into what the founder is building
- Express authentic alignment with their mission (financial empowerment, community impact)

Generic messages get ignored. Avoid:

- "Let's connect on LinkedIn" with no context
- "Any time to chat?" without specific purpose
- Mass-templated outreach

### The "Give First" Mentality

Startup founders operate on reciprocity. Effective approaches:

- Ask for mentorship rather than jobs directly
- Offer specific skills or feedback before requesting opportunities
- Show willingness to learn rather than immediate compensation

Founders who made mistakes building companies want to help others avoid them, creating openness to genuine learners.

## Career Pivoting Strategies

### Translating Skills Across Industries

Transferable skills matter more than direct experience. For career changers:

- Use human intelligence to authentically explain skill translation
- Focus on how current capabilities apply to new role requirements
- Avoid AI-generated generic translations that recruiters recognize

### Resume and LinkedIn Optimization

The summary section is critical for career pivoters. Best practices:

- State exactly what jobs you're seeking in the top summary
- Prevent mismatched outreach by clarifying you're changing careers
- Tell your story rather than relying on standardized bullet points

AI-generated resumes are detectable. While no formal detection tool exists, recruiters identify:

- Overly standardized language
- Generic AI-friendly phrasing
- Lack of authentic personal voice

## Navigating AI in Job Search

### The AI Paradox

Employers expect AI proficiency but want authentic applications. The tension:

- Startups need candidates who can use AI to increase productivity
- Resume reviewers can identify AI-generated content
- Interview tools now provide AI prompts during Zoom calls

### Standing Out in an AI Era

Human relationship-building remains irreplaceable. Strategies:

- Attend offline job fairs and meet people in person
- Have real conversations about past experience and current interests
- Network through alumni communities and Fordham platforms like RAM Connect

Storytelling and authenticity differentiate candidates. Move beyond standardized STAR method questions to share genuine personal narratives.

## Unique Value Proposition Framework

### Everyone Has One

Your unique value proposition is like a fingerprint. Young professionals often underestimate their distinctiveness. Key principle: Enter every conversation thinking "What value can I authentically give?"

### Managing Expectations

Low response rates are normal. If 100 outreach attempts yield 3 responses, focus on those 3 rather than the 97 non-responses. Each positive response represents someone who saw your authentic value.

### Intent-Driven Communication

Display your unique value with clear intent. Whether seeking mentorship, advice, or employment, articulate how you'll contribute to what the other person is building.
